Travels Abroad

Key West

Hemingway initially stopped in Key West, Florida, on his return home from Paris. He and his second wife, Pauline, rented an apartment on an island there for several years before eventually purchasing a home. Key West gave Hemingway the opportunity to fish and sail on the open waters.

Africa

Hemingway and his wife Pauline traveled to Africa for a three-month safari in the summer of 1933, where he spent his time hunting game and enjoying time outdoors. Ernest’s experience in Africa inspired later literary works such as The Green Hills of Africa, The Snows of Kilimanjaro, and The Short Happy Life of Francis Macomber. Hemingway would journey to Africa many years later with his fourth wife for another excursion, and it is at this time that Hemingway’s mythical adventurousness and fearlessness really took shape. He was in two plane crashes on his second trip to the continent, but he miraculously survived them both.

Spain

Spain was perhaps one of the most important places to Hemingway, both for his literary career and his psychological and emotional being. Spain provided a setting for five of his major books – The Sun Also Rises(1926), Death in the Afternoon (1932), For Whom the Bell Tolls (1940), The Dangerous Summer (1985), and The Garden of Eden (1986). It also provided inspiration for numerous vignettes and short stories, and was incidentally where his only full-length play, The Fifth Column (1938).

During Hemingway’s numerous visits to Spain in the 1920’s and 1930’s, he learned a tremendous amount about Spain’s culture and history. However, Hemingway’s greatest area of interest was in the Spanish bullfighting. Hemingway’s Death in the Afternoon was praised by many Spanish bullfighting enthusiasts, despite his work not gaining much recognition in Spain until it was published much later.

The Spanish Civil War

Hemingway was extremely invested in Spain and her people, and he took interest when fascism spread throughout Europe and as Spain broke out into a civil war. Hemingway had encountered fascism briefly in the 1920s when he had the chance to interview Mussolini, and he saw him plainly as the brutal dictator he would later become.

At the time, Francisco Franco led a group of Spanish nationalists in a revolt against the Spanish government, and Hemingway had the opportunity to cover the unfolding events. It was during this time that he worked with the famous war photographer Robert Capa, as well as fellow journalist Martha Gellhorn, who would later become his third wife.

Cuba

Hemingway moved to Havana, Cuba in 1940 with his third wife Martha Gellhorn, where he would live for the next twenty years. He fished aboard his boat and enjoyed living on the island. Hemingway did a great deal of writing during his time living in Cuba, and many of his works from the1940s were edited and published posthumously. Hemingway still worked as a reporter during his time spent living in Cuba. Before he went to Europe to cover World War II, Hemingway armed his boat with weaponry and radio communications, and patrolled the Caribbean for German U-Boats.

WWII Coverage

Hemingway covered many key events in the Second World War, including the D-Day landings where he accompanied American soldiers as a civilian correspondent. Hemingway also got into trouble attempting to lead a group of resistance people in the gathering of military intelligence in the village of Rambouillet. Despite this, he was awarded a Bronze Star for his services and bravery during the war.

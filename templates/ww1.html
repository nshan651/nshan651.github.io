WWI

Preparing to go to War

Shortly after his six-month tenure as a reporter for the Kansas City Star, Hemingway served in the First World War as a volunteer ambulance driver with the American Red Cross. After hearing that the Red Cross was looking for volunteer ambulance drivers, Hemingway reached out to Theodore Brumback. Brumback was the son of a prominent judge, and he had spent five months as an ambulance driver in the countryside of northern France.

Hemingway resolved to serve and finished his tenure with the Star in late April 1918. Hemingway returned home to Oak Park briefly, all the while corresponding with Brumback about their plans to serve in Italy. Hemingway, Brumback, and several other volunteers traveled to New York where they spent two weeks training and sight-seeing. They traveled across the Atlantic by steamship and arrived in France where they made stops in Bordeaux and Paris. Hemingway finally arrived in Milan, Italy, in early June 1918.

The Horrors of War

Hemingway’s cohort of volunteers were given an immediate assignment upon arrival. Hemingway was sent to the site of a munitions plant explosion just outside of Milan. They arrived at a gruesome scene where bodies and various body parts were strewn about. Hemingway would later describe the horrific picture as a “baptism of fire” in the short story A Natural History of the Dead.

After this, Hemingway and Brumback were split up, and Hemingway was sent to Schio, 150 miles northeast of Milan. Hemingway was there for several weeks but saw very little action and expressed a sense of boredom because of a lack of things to do. When an opportunity arose later in June, Hemingway left his ambulance unit and took over a mobile canteen operation near the villages of Fornaci and Fossalta.

A Brush with Death

Hemingway’s work with the canteen involved distributing coffee, chocolate, cigarettes, and post cards to soldiers in the trenches. One night in early June, Hemingway was badly injured by Austrian mortar fire that landed just feet away from him. Hemingway was later awarded the Silver Medal of Valor from the Italian government after carrying a wounded Italian soldier to safety and getting injured again by machine-gun fire.

Due to his grievous injuries, Hemingway spent the remainder of the war recovering at the American Red Cross hospital in Milan. Hemingway later described the event in a letter: “Then there was a flash, as when a blast-furnace door is swung open, and a roar that started white and went red.”

A Fiery Love Affair

During his six-month recovery in Milan, Hemingway fell deeply in love with his nurse, Agnes von Kurowsky. Their passionate relationship lasted two weeks after the Armistice, after which Hemingway returned to the states. Hemingway’s relationship and physical rehabilitation would serve as inspiration for several of his future novels, most notably A Fairwell to Arms.

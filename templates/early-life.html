Early Life

Humble Beginnings

Ernest Miller Hemingway was born in Oak Park, Illinois, a middle-class suburb of Chicago. Hemingway was raised, according to the Hemingway Society, with the strong Midwestern values of hard work, religion and faith, physical well-being, and self-determination. These are the values that would help Hemingway prepare for both the horrors of war and his future writing career.

Hemingway would spend the first twenty years of his life here, and it is in these early years that Hemingway would develop his admiration for the fine arts and the outdoors. Hemingway’s father taught him to have a deeper reverence for nature and the outdoors. His mother fostered his love for the fine arts by taking him to Chicago opera houses and museums. Both parents were insistent on raising him in the protestant faith (although he would later convert to Catholicism after marrying his second wife). Hemingway became very involved in the church by joining the choir and reflecting on sermons.

Hemingway and the Great Outdoors

Hemingway spent the summers with his family in the woods of Northern Michigan at the family’s summer home on Walloon Lake. Young Hemingway would explore the land, fish, and hunt small game. Hunting and fishing remained passions of his throughout his life. Hemingway’s boyhood experiences at Horton Bay and Walloon Lake would continue to inspire his writing, despite his adventures around the world.

"All the love went into fishing and the summer. He had loved it more than anything. He had loved digging potatoes with Bill in the fall . . . . The hills at the foot of Walloon Lake, storms on the lake. . . delivering vegetables around the lake. . . coming up from the foot of the lake with groceries, the mail and the Chicago paper under a tarpaulin. . . . the wind in the hemlocks and the wet pine needles underfoot when he was barefoot going for the milk. Getting up at daylight to row across the lake and hike over the hills after a rain to fish in Hortons Creek.”
-From “On Writing,” The Nick Adams Stories by Ernest Hemingway, 1972

Scholar, Athelete, and Journalist

Hemingway excelled both academically and athletically. He demonstrated a particular talent for writing, writing for both his school newspaper, Trapeze, and for the yearbook. He published some of his very first writings under the pen name Ring Lardner, Jr.

After he finished high school, Hemingway chose to forgo higher education in favor of pursuing his writing career. His first job was as a reporter for the Kansas City Star, which, although short, would go on to influence Hemingway’s future writing style. He would go on to adopt short, declarative statements in his writing. This was relatively uncommon at a time when American writing was heavily influenced by lavish, adjective-rich prose.

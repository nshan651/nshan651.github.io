Paris

A Hero's Return

After the war, Hemingway returned to the States and was hailed as a hero. He took a position as a foreign correspondent writing for the Toronto Star. It was at this time that he made the commitment to write fiction, and several of his experimental short stories drew the attention of well-known author Sherwood Anderson. Anderson gave him a letter of introduction to two well-known American authors living in Europe at the time, Gertrude Stein and Ezra Pound. Hemingway was living in Chicago at this time and had just been married to Hadley Richardson. At the urging of Sherwood Anderson, the two of them decided to move to Paris.

The Lost Generation

After Hemingway moved to Paris, he was introduced to the “Parisian Modern Movement” of literature. Hemingway became a part of the “Lost Generation,” a literary group whose members included James Joyce, Max Eastman, Gertrude Stein, Ezra Pound, F. Scott Fitzgerald, and T.S. Elliot. Hemingway learned a great deal from this talented group of authors, and his years in Paris were some of the happiest and most productive of his life. Hemingway’s posthumously published collection of memoirs, titled A Moveable Feast, describe his time in Paris.

Hemingway's Writing in Paris

Hemingway published his first book, Three Stories & Ten Poems in Paris in 1923. His next book With In Our Time followed in 1925, which drew from Hemingway’s childhood experiences in Michigan as a source of inspiration. Hemingway also did a fair amount of work for the Toronto Star during his time spent abroad. He covered several world events such as the Geneva Conference in 1922 and the Greco-Turkish War.

He returned to the US in 1926 with the manuscript for The Sun Also Rises, which he published shortly after his return. During this time, his relationship with his wife began to deteriorate, and he had an affair with Vogue magazine editor Pauline Pfeiffer which ultimately led to him divorcing Hadley. Hemingway and Pfeiffer got married shortly after.
